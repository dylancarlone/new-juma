Before the first pitch was thrown in what would become the first Opening Night in Major League Baseball history, 80 young people were already at work.

They were behind the counters, on the concourse, and at the stands throughout Oracle Park — Juma youth, showing up the way they always do: early, ready, and eager to prove themselves. On March 25, as the San Francisco Giants welcomed the New York Yankees to open the 2026 MLB season in prime time, Juma youth were part of the experience for every fan who walked through the gates. Two days later, they did it again — and then some.

On Opening Day, Juma employed 124 youth across two cities and two stadiums, the highest single-day participation in our organization’s history. 110 youth worked Oracle Park for the Giants’ home opener, while fourteen others were at T-Mobile Park in Seattle, serving Mariners fans on one of baseball’s most celebrated days. It was a new record, and the numbers behind it tell the story.

Juma youth at Oracle Park alone logged a combined 620 hours of work, earned $15,500 in wages, and served an average of 300 guests each. The Peet’s Coffee and Tea stand behind the scoreboard — run entirely by Juma youth — went through roughly 300 pounds of ice during Opening Day, working to keep pace with a steady stream of fans ordering iced lattes, iced matcha, and iced tea. Every Dippin’ Dots stand at Oracle Park is operated exclusively by Juma youth, and we also run stands serving Boba Tea, espresso drinks, and one of the park’s more beloved novelties: baseball glove-shaped waffles with Oreos. These aren’t peripheral roles. They are high-volume, customer-facing positions in one of the most visited venues in the Bay Area, on its busiest days of the year.

For many of the youth who worked Opening Week, it was their first shift at Juma — and their first job ever. For others, it was another chapter in a longer story that Juma has been helping them write, one paycheck and one coaching session at a time. Juma’s model pairs real employment with career-development workshops and individualized coaching, giving young people not just a job, but a foundation for what comes next. Working a Giants home opener or a Mariners game isn’t just a line on a resume. It’s evidence — to employers, to mentors, and to themselves — that they can perform under pressure, serve with professionalism, and show up when it counts.

Opening Week 2026 offered all of that, 124 times over.
